Lab instructions

Practical advice for the work in the wet lab



Due to the corona pandemic, our actual laboratory work could only start in the beginning of June. Even though we had prepared ourselves as best as possible by doing research and looking for protocols, we had to face various challenges and problems. With the following definitions of terms, tips and laboratory instructions, we would like to ease the lab preparation of future teams, so that more time can be invested in what is really important: the project.
Glossary
Autoclaving
Sterilization of culture media and laboratory materials through thermal treatment in overpressure range using gas-tight, lockable pressure vessels. The aim is not to guarantee the killing of germs, but to reduce the probability of contamination of the sterile goods to 10^-6 cells. Moist heat even enables bacterial spores to be killed by swelling, which reduces their resistance. The required time interval t is different depending on the germ.
Centrifugation
Separation process that uses the principle of inertia. The sedimentation speed depends on the relative centrifugal force, in most cases a multiple of the gravitational constant g.
Clean Bench
Laminar flow work cabinet or similar workspaces that provide filtered air across the work surface to protect against contamination. The clean bench protects the work on the work surface (e.g. when creating microorganism cultures), but not the user or the environment from aerosols created on the work surface.
Competence
Ability of cells to take up DNA that is freely present in the surrounding medium; it is therefore a prerequisite for the transformation of bacteria. Only certain types of bacteria show natural competence. Various treatment methods (e.g. using heat, chemicals or electricity) also enable artificial competence to be achieved.
Cryostock
Storage of cells or tissues by freezing, e.g. in liquid nitrogen. Due to the resulting cold rigor and the resulting greatly slowed down metabolic processes, it is possible to maintain the vitality of the cells/tissues almost indefinitely.
Culture
Scheduled artificial cultivation and reproduction of microorganisms and viruses as well as plant, animal and human cells or tissues under controlled conditions. Common culture processes and methods: pure culture, mixed culture, nutrient medium culture, liquid culture, stitch culture, enrichment culture, selective culture, fermentation.
Dialysis
Physical process for separation of low molecular weight substances from solutions of macromolecular/colloidal compounds through the exclusive diffusion of the low molecular weight substances through a semipermeable membrane by using a concentration gradient.
Generation time
Doubling time; Time span in which the number of cells doubled in microorganisms under growth conditions. Approximate guide values under optimal conditions:
  • Bacteria: 15-60 min
  • unicellular Eukaryotes (e.g. yeasts): 90-120 min
Innoculation
Targeted transfer of viable cells/microorganisms (inoculum, see Preculture) in a larger nutrient medium or an organism for the purpose of reproduction.
Insert
DNA fragment that was ligated into cloning vector.
Lysis
Cell disruption (or homogenization) through (active) damage or dissolution of the outer cell membrane using mechanical (e.g. French Press, Ultrasonic, Vortex, mortar) or chemical (e.g. hypotonic buffers, Lysozyme and Triton X-100) methods or means. The result is called the lysate.
Medium
Nutrient or culture media are used for the controlled cultivation of microorganisms, cells, tissues or small plants with a certain supply of nutrients. A distinction is made between liquid and gelled (‚Äúsolid‚Äù) media. With the addition of antibiotics or special substrates, additional selection can be made.
Molecular Cloning
Methods for obtaining and identical amplification of DNA by integrating the desired DNA fragment into a vector (e.g. a plasmid). The use of various organisms (e.g. Escherichia coli, algae, fungi) as hosts enables a cost-effective and precise alternative to in vitro methods (e.g. PCR), which also enables the production of large quantities.

Functional cloning identifies similar DNA sequences, positional cloning identifies neighbouring DNA sequences.
pH meter
Measuring device for determining and displaying the pH value of a solution. The determination takes place via electrochemical means, in the lab often with the help of a glass electrode.
Plasmid
Small, usually circular, autonomously replicating, double-stranded DNA molecules that do not belong to actual chromosome and are found in bacteria and archaea (rare in eukaryotes). The genes contained on plasmids can provide a selection advantage (e.g. antibiotic resistance). Each plasmid contains at least one sequence that serves as the origin of replication (ORI) and must be compatible with the strain of microorganism to enable replication of the plasmid. Different types of plasmids can coexist in one and the same cell (number depends on the organism in question). Due to their properties, they can be used as vectors to duplicate or express genes. The specific adaptation of the plasmid as a vector is known as vector design.
Transformation
In addition to transduction and conjugation, transformation is one of three options for gene transfer in prokaryotes. Free DNA is taken up by naturally or artificially competent cells. It is used as a substep in molecular cloning after the vector insert construct has been designed. In this way, the desired DNA section (the insert) can be duplicated very frequently and inexpensively.
Preculture
Process for production of inoculation material (inoculum) as preparation for cell reproduction on a large scale by means of stepwise enlargement in standing or shaking cultures.
General tips for (first) time in the lab
  • Get a lab book and immediately document everything you do.
  • Schedule a weekly meeting for the lab team to review ‚Äì talk to each other!
  • Find a common sharing platform (e.g. Microsoft Teams).
  • As soon as they are established, write down your experiments directly in the form of a protocol and update them regularly if necessary.
  • Check early before the start of an experiment whether all the necessary materials are available so that you can obtain/order them in good time.
  • Seriously: talk to each other! About everything ‚Äì what you do, what you want to do, what is there, what is missing‚Ä¶
  • Always have a ‚Äúwaste beaker‚Äù ready at the workplace for used media, liquid cultures that are no longer required, residual chemicals, etc.
  • Ask for help if you are not sure.
  • Label everything you have (bottles with media, tubes, agar plates, etc.) with at least name of content, date, (group) name.
  • Prepare a negative control in order to exclude contamination by medium or materials (e.g. when preparing precultures).

Clean Bench

Prepare sterile workingplace

  1. Disinfection working surface with surface disinfectant.
  2. Prepare a waste bag.
  3. Have all the materials you need for your work ready, e.g. micropipettes, sterile tips, media, tubes, beakers, petri dishes, etc.
  4. Sterilize them by surface disinfection when placing in Clean Bench.
  5. Have paper towels within reach.
  6. To reduce contamination, disinfect the tip of your micropipette.
  7. Wait about 15 min before actually starting work at Clean Bench, so everything inside gets decontaminated and sterilized by laminar flow.
  8. Label everything while waiting.
  9. Disinfect your working surface again when starting with work.
  10. Disinfect gloves.
  11. When you are done, clean up the working space and disinfect the working surface.

Pipetting techniques

  • for usage of modern pipettes, like micropipettes
  • tips are normally for single usage

Tips and tricks

  • best results by using tips offered/recommended by manufacturer of pipette
  • when aspirating the medium, hold pipette in as vertical position as possible
    → volume deviations can occure as angle of inclination increases
  • pre-wetting the tip improves pipetting accuracy in all techniques by rising the humidity in tip
    → causes less evaporation
  • stripping of droplets adhering to tip avoids transfer of additional liquid
  • stack two tips on top of each other to make it longer and reach even into deep vessels

Cell harvest

Material

  • Cell culture
  • 50 mL falcon tube
  • Buffer or medium
  • Waste beaker

Procedure

  1. Aliquot cell culture to 50 mL falcon tubes.
  2. Set centrifuge to 4¬∞C.
  3. Centrifuge at 4¬∞C, 4000 rpm for 10 min.
  4. Decant supernatant carefully.
  5. Resuspend pellet in 2 mL buffer or medium.
  6. Combine all resuspended pellets in one falcon.
  7. Centrifuge again at 4¬∞C, 4000 rpm for 10 min.
  8. Carefully decant supernatant.
  9. Store pellet at -20¬∞C or further use immediately.

Centrifugation

Tips

  • Due to the different types of centrifuges and centrifugation methods, a briefing and extensive examination of the necessary properties is essential for the success of an experiment.
  • Even with small benchtop centrifuges, make sure that the rotor loaded with the samples is balanced in that opposing tubes have identical weights. The bigger and faster the centrifuge, the more important.
  • If the number of sample tubes is odd, use a tube with water with the same weight as its counterpart.

Lysis

Tips

  • Larger pieces of tissue should first be crushed in the mixer.
  • Physical processes often lead to an increase in temperature ‚Äì control and possible cooling is necessary.
  • Chemical processes are usually gentler than mechanical ones.

pH meter

In general

  • Electrode is always stored in KCl solution (to leave diaphragm potential neutral and conductive) and should never get dry!
  • Always control if pH meter measures correct pH of KCl solution before usage. If not: exchange solution.
  • pH electrode: lever to open and close √† open to measure
  • Always rinse electrode with distilled water before measuring another solution/storing it again in KCl.
  • If you think you rinsed enough ‚Äì rinse a little bit more üòâ. It provides contamination of solutions.
  • Best always calibrate pH meter before measuring actual solution.
  • Always stir liquid while measuring/adjusting it.
  • Never hit electrode with stirring rod!
  • Always wait a few seconds until the pH reading is constant.

Measuring

  1. Take electrode out of KCl solution and rinse it with distilled water (into waste beaker).
  2. Attach electrode to setup by fixing in needed position with clamp.
  3. Tip of electrode with diaphragm has to be submerged in liquid.
  4. Calibration with solutions in acidic, neutral and basic range (e.g. pH 4, 7 and 9):
    • Combination of calibration solutions depends on pH you want to measure/you want to adjust to.
    • Calibrate with solutions that enclose the desired pH (e.g.: solution to be measured: 7,5 ‚Äì calibration with 7 and 9 (because 7,5 is in between)).
    • Always stir solutions before measuring.
    • Always start with ph 7, rinse electrode and calibrate the other value.
    • If needed, adjust display at pH meter as specified by manufacturer.
  5. Rinse electrode off.
  6. Measure pH value of solution.
  7. If needed, adjust pH with acid or base.
  8. Rinse the electrode off when you‚Äôre done.
  9. Store in KCl solution.

Dialysis

Tips

  • The larger the surface area of the membrane in relation to the liquid to be dialyzed, the faster the dialysis will proceed.
  • A continuous or at least frequent exchange of the solvent increases the dialysis speed by maintaining the concentration gradient.

Cryostock

Storage of cells or tissues by freezing, e.g. in liquid nitrogen. Due to the resulting cold rigor and the resulting greatly slowed down metabolic processes, it is possible to maintain the vitality of the cells/tissues almost indefinitely.

Materials

  • Cell Culture
  • 70% glycerol
  • Tube with screw cap
  • Micropipette + tips
  • (liquid nitrogen)

Procedure

  1. Mix 500 ¬µL of culture with 500 ¬µL of 70% glycerol in a tube with screw cap by gently pipetting up and down.
  2. Freeze in liquid nitrogen or store directly at -80¬∞C.

Inoculation

Tips

  • Workplace: Clean Bench
  • Always use fresh, sufficiently large inoculum with good cell density (to reach phase of exponential growth).
  • The nutrient medium should always be fresh and contain all the necessary growth substances.
  • Pay attention to optimal environmental conditions (e.g. temperature, ventilation, light, etc.).

I1-60GFPHis

ATPase Inhibitor with GFP and His-Tag

The first of these parts is BBa_K3456000. It is a composite part coding for the first 60 residues of the bovine ATPase inhibitor IF1 followed by an eGFP- and a His-Tag. By binding to the ATPase, it inhibits the hydrolytic function of the ATPase but not the ATP synthesis. For this purpose, only the first 60 amino acid residues of this protein are needed. The whole part can be used to purify an ATPase with an affinity purification via the His-Tag. Its expression is regulated by a Lac-Operon consisting of LacI, LacO and a T7 promoter and terminator.
I1-60

ATPase Inhibitor

The second part, whose sequence has been contributed to the part registry, is the basic coding part BBa_K3456001. The sequence of the eGFP- and the His-Tag have already been entered in the parts registry. We only needed to add the sequence coding for the first 60 residues of IF1 in order to be able to create the composite part BBa_K3456000. These first 60 residues of IF1 have been uploaded as the basic coding part BBa_K3456001.
MBP-1

Polystyrene-binding peptide

The third sequence we contributed to the parts registry (BBa_K3456002) codes for MBP-1, which is an antimicrobial peptide originating from Zea mays (Maize). It can bind to polystyrene-coated magnetic particles. Thus, we planned to use it as an anchor peptide that connects the polystyrene beads to our linker peptide. We used this part in the composite part BBa_K3456004.
Domain Z

Stiff spacer domain

The fourth part whose sequence we entered in the parts registry codes for the stiff spacer Domain Z. Its part name is BBa_K3456003. We used this domain to connect the anchor peptides MBP1 and LCI. Thus, it is also a basic part for the composite part BBa_K3456004 coding for the MBP-1 - LCI fusion protein.
MBP-1 - LCI fusion protein

Connector between polystyrene coated magnetic particles and liposomes

The composite part BBa_K3456004 is the fifth and last part whose sequence we contributed to the parts registry. It codes for the MBP-1 - LCI fusion protein, which can connect polystyrene-coated magnetic particles and liposomes consisting of 16:0-18:1 PC (POPC)(1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-glycero-3-phosphocholine). It consists of the anchor peptide MBP-1, the stiff spacer Domain Z and the anchor peptide LCI. We already found the sequence of the LCI peptide in the parts registry. The sequences of MBP1 and the spacer Domain Z have been contributed by us.
