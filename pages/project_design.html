Process of designing M.A.R.S.
Enzyme cascades are the future of production. We can not afford to continue the energy intensive polluting and wasteful production of chemicals and pharmaceuticalsâ€¦

By using enzymes much less energy needs to be invested in the catalysis of a chemical reaction. Still enzymes depend a very specific energy source to operate. It is the molecule ATP. The only enzyme, which generates ATP in a significant amount is the ATP-Synthase. In vivo it is located in the inner mitrochondrial membrane in eukaryotes or in the cytoplasma membrane of procaryotes. The ATP-Synthase uses a protongradient and converts an electrochemical potential into chemical energy. This protongradient is in vivo generated by the use of high energy electrons to pump protons via respiratory complexes from one side of the membrane to the other. Besides the protonpumping the respiratory complexes 1,3 and 4 have other tasks which are needed to keep a living cell operating. To simplify the respiratory process membrane proteins that generate a protongradient and the ATP-Synthase which can utilze the gradient are needed. This system resembles A minimal mitochondria.

To generate a protongradient we did not chose an respiratory complex, but bacteriorhodopsin. Bacteriorhodopsin is a light dependent ionchannel and does not require high energy electrons but light energy as power source. Integrating these two membrane proteins into a polymersome enables the generation of ATP from ADP and anorganic phosphate, just by using light energy, which is the easiest and cleanest energy available. The next step after figuring the basic design out was the optimisation by making the whole system reusable by connecting these powerplants with magnetic particels using anchor peptides. So that after the use of M.A.R.S our polymersomes can be separated and used again.
